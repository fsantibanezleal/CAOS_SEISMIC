\begin{abstract}
Operational earthquake forecasting (OEF) issues calibrated conditional probabilities
of future seismicity; the Epidemic-Type Aftershock Sequence (ETAS) model is its
de-facto benchmark, and under fair, prospective, CSEP-style testing no machine-learning
temporal point process has been shown to beat a well-fit ETAS. We describe a complete,
honesty-first OEF system and, on it, a leakage-free, multi-region CSEP testbed built to
answer, rather than assert, four questions. The system fits a regime-tiled space--time
ETAS with a full hygiene pipeline (rolling magnitude of completeness, moment-magnitude
homogenization, dual-catalog declustering, propagated uncertainty), against a mandatory
adaptive smoothed-seismicity Poisson null and a transparent Reasenberg--Jones fallback,
calibrated by isotonic regression with a genuine epistemic-plus-aleatory uncertainty
triad, and it emits both gridded and catalog-based forecast representations so that
over-dispersion is scored honestly. Skill is established only by winning CSEP comparison
tests (information gain per earthquake, IGPE, in nats) against both baselines, under a
strict forecast clock that engineers against five leakage modes, with pre-registered
ship rules and shuffled-label negative controls. Four artifact-backed findings result.
(i) ETAS adds skill over the stationary null only where there is triggering to exploit
(Japan $+0.072$ nats at one day; low-seismicity interiors exactly $0.0$), quantifying
the high-versus-low-seismicity bias. (ii) A convex log-score-optimal stack of
ETAS-family variants earns a real, significant \emph{global} gain ($+0.011$ over 751
events) that does not generalize to the canonical active margins; under the
pre-registered rule it is a no-ship, and a temporally adaptive variant is refuted as a
multiple-comparison artifact. (iii) The binding one-to-seven-day consistency failure is
count over-dispersion, not spatial shape: a catalog-based number test passes a window the
Poisson number test rejects, and the frozen-intensity forecast systematically under-counts
by about $28\%$ because it omits within-window secondary triggering. (iv) The value of a
GNSS-strain geodetic context covariate, added to a Hawkes-structured neural point process,
is \emph{horizon-dependent}: it does not beat ETAS at the one-to-seven-day operational
horizon (mean IGPE $-0.053$ over eight weekly windows) but beats it robustly at thirty days
(global $+0.115$ over 2166 events, positive in $9/10$ windows and in every high-seismicity
region), because the calibrated model deploys a time-flat geodetic background rather than
triggering. We conclude that base tiled ETAS is at or near the practical ceiling for
mean-rate one-to-seven-day IGPE over global $M\!\ge\!5$, and that the honest place for a
geodetic covariate is a longer, background-dominated outlook.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% =====================================================================

\IEEEPARstart{E}{arthquakes} cannot be predicted deterministically: whether a small
rupture cascades into a great earthquake depends on unmeasurably fine details of the
crust~\cite{geller1997}. What can be done, and is done operationally, is
\emph{forecasting}: issuing a probability strictly in $(0,1)$ for a region, a magnitude
band, and a short horizon. Following the ICEF definition~\cite{jordan2011}, a prediction
is a deterministic statement that an event will or will not occur, whereas a forecast
reports a probability with uncertainty; short-term probabilities of a large event may
vary over orders of magnitude relative to background yet typically remain small in
absolute terms (well under a few percent per day). This distinction is not pedantic. The
documented harm in the field is \emph{false reassurance}, so every number here is a
conditional probability shown next to its long-term baseline, scored prospectively, and
never rendered as an alarm.

The de-facto short-term baseline is the Epidemic-Type Aftershock Sequence
model~\cite{ogata1988,ogata1998}, a self-exciting Hawkes point process. Operational
systems such as the USGS aftershock forecast~\cite{reasenberg1989,page2016} and
OEF-Italy~\cite{spassiani2023} run ETAS-class models as scheduled services; a decade of
prospective next-day forecasting over California confirms that no single model dominates,
with the short-term-clustering models excelling during sequences and ETAS the consistent
generalist~\cite{serafini2025}. Against this baseline, machine learning has repeatedly
promised more and, under fair testing, repeatedly failed to deliver a prospective gain:
the EarthquakeNPP benchmark~\cite{stockman2026} found that none of five modern neural
point processes beat ETAS once strict chronological splits and CSEP consistency tests are
imposed. Where learned models can help, the gains come from two ETAS gaps, ingesting
covariates ETAS cannot easily absorb, and learning spatial anisotropy, not from network
depth~\cite{dascher2023,zlydenko2023}.

This paper makes two kinds of contribution. First, it documents a complete, honesty-first
OEF system: the estimator stack (Section~\ref{sec:models}), its calibration and
uncertainty, and a leakage-free, multi-region CSEP evaluation protocol
(Section~\ref{sec:eval}) with a strict forecast clock, pre-registered ship rules, and
shuffled-label negative controls. Second, on that testbed it reports four artifact-backed
findings (Section~\ref{sec:results}), each with its honest negative kept on the record: the
high-versus-low-seismicity bias of ETAS; an ETAS-family stack that gains globally but not
regionally; the over-dispersion and secondary-cascade nature of the binding short-horizon
consistency failure; and the horizon-dependent value of a GNSS-strain geodetic covariate.
Every number is reconciled to a committed, reproducible artifact.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}
% =====================================================================

The catalog spine is USGS ComCat (FDSN event service, public domain), homogenized to
moment magnitude $M_w$. Where a per-network station-pair regression to $M_w$ is
unavailable, the Scordilis~\cite{scordilis2006} global $m_b\!\to\!M_w$ and $M_s\!\to\!M_w$
orthogonal-regression relations are used as a documented fallback with per-event
provenance; this recovers a six-fold larger conditioning catalog (from $1.65\times10^4$
to $9.7\times10^4$ events, $M\!\ge\!4.5$, 1990--2026) at the cost of a bounded, audited
$b$-value inflation that the per-tile fit largely removes (a naive global $b\approx1.59$
becomes a per-tile production $b\approx1.34$). The magnitude of completeness $M_c$ and the
Gutenberg--Richter $b$-value are estimated on rolling windows with their
uncertainty~\cite{wiemer2000,woessner2005}, the Aki--Utsu $b$ carrying its Shi--Bolt
standard error~\cite{shi1982}. Declustering, where the stationary background requires it,
uses stochastic declustering~\cite{zhuang2002} and the nearest-neighbour
method~\cite{zaliapin2013}; the scored target, however, is the \emph{non-declustered}
catalog, because clustering is what we forecast. The geodetic covariate is the GNSS strain
rate from the Nevada Geodetic Laboratory MIDAS velocity field~\cite{blewitt2016}
(20{,}168 stations), gridded to a coarse global field; strain is high on active margins
(Japan 95, California 154, Chile 64 nstrain/yr) and low in stable interiors (Central US
3.6, Western Europe 5.6). Plate-boundary geometry is PB2002~\cite{bird2003}. Raw catalogs,
features, and model weights are rebuildable from the code and provenance manifests and are
not versioned; the compact daily and back-analysis artifacts are.

\section{Models: the estimator stack}
\label{sec:models}
% =====================================================================

Every model implements one seam, the \texttt{Forecaster} port
\texttt{fit}$\to$\texttt{expected\_counts}, so the null, the reference, and any challenger
are fit on the identical conditioning slice and scored on identical bins.

\subsection{The public target: a bounded exceedance probability}

The single number a user sees is the probability of at least one event above a target
magnitude $M^\ast$ in a cell over a horizon $[0,T]$, a non-homogeneous Poisson exceedance
probability
\begin{equation}
P(\ge 1\ \text{event}\ge M^\ast) = 1 - e^{-N_{\ge M^\ast}}, \quad
N_{\ge M^\ast}=\!\int_0^T\!\!\!\int_A\!\lambda\,\Phi(M^\ast)\,dx\,dy\,dt,
\end{equation}
where the magnitude term is the Gutenberg--Richter tail fraction, \emph{bounded} by a
per-region maximum magnitude $M_{\max}$ so an unbounded tail can never inflate the
rare-event probability,
\begin{equation}
\Phi(M^\ast)=\frac{10^{-b(M^\ast-M_c)}-10^{-b(M_{\max}-M_c)}}{1-10^{-b(M_{\max}-M_c)}},
\quad M_c\le M^\ast\le M_{\max}.
\end{equation}
The public formula $P=1-e^{-N}$ never changes; only the quality of the conditional
intensity $\lambda$ improves. The full conditional magnitude distribution is forecast (not
a single threshold), which is what enables the CSEP magnitude test and keeps the display
region-appropriate.

\subsection{The mandatory null: adaptive smoothed seismicity}

A stationary Poisson estimate of where earthquakes occur, obtained by smoothing a
declustered catalog with the Helmstetter--Kagan--Jackson adaptive power-law
kernel~\cite{helmstetter2007},
\begin{equation}
\mu(x,y)=\sum_i K_{d_i}(r_i),\qquad K_d(r)=C(d)\,(r^2+d^2)^{-s},
\end{equation}
with bandwidth $d_i$ the distance to event $i$'s $n$-th nearest neighbour. It is both the
spatial background that seeds ETAS and the mandatory stationary reference any
time-dependent model must beat. The exponent $s$ and normalization $C(d)$ are pinned to
named reference profiles, never assumed.

\subsection{The primary estimator: space--time ETAS}

ETAS is a self-exciting Hawkes process, a stationary background plus the summed, decaying
offspring of every past event~\cite{ogata1998},
\begin{equation}
\lambda(t,x,y\mid\mathcal H_t)=\mu(x,y)+\!\!\sum_{i:\,t_i<t}\!\! K\,e^{\alpha(M_i-M_0)}
\Big(1+\tfrac{t-t_i}{c}\Big)^{-p} f(x-x_i,y-y_i\mid M_i),
\end{equation}
with Utsu productivity $k(m)=K e^{\alpha(m-M_0)}$, the Omori--Utsu kernel
$g(t)=\tfrac{p-1}{c}(1+t/c)^{-p}$, and the Ogata inverse-power spatial kernel
$f(r\mid M_i)=\tfrac{q-1}{\pi\zeta^2}(1+r^2/\zeta^2)^{-q}$, $\zeta=De^{\gamma(M_i-M_0)}$.
Parameters $(K,\alpha,c,p,D,\gamma,q)$ are fit by maximum likelihood on the full
un-declustered catalog, maximizing the point-process log-likelihood
\begin{equation}
\ln L=\sum_i\ln\lambda(t_i,x_i,y_i\mid\mathcal H_{t_i})-\!\int_0^T\!\!\!\int_A\!\lambda\,dx\,dy\,dt.
\end{equation}
Two distinct stability gates are enforced: finite branching ($\alpha<\beta$, $\beta=b\ln10$)
and subcriticality (branching ratio $n<1$); a supercritical fit signals a mis-fit and is
rejected. Because a single global ETAS over a $10^5$-event catalog is intractable and
physically wrong (subduction and stable interior obey different kinematics), we fit a
\emph{regime-tiled} ETAS: 195 ETAS tiles are fit and 265 sparse tiles fall back to their
smoothed null, at $M_c=5.35$, $b=1.337$.

\subsection{The transparent fallback: Reasenberg--Jones}

The most transparent aftershock model~\cite{reasenberg1989,page2016} conditions on the
largest triggering event before issue time, with rate
$\lambda(t,M)=10^{\,a+b(M_m-M)}(t+c)^{-p}$ distributed over cells by a Wells--Coppersmith
rupture-length kernel~\cite{wells1994}; its constants are flagged as California-derived and
never silently reused for Chilean subduction. It is a human-auditable cross-check, not the
primary spatial forecaster.

\subsection{The gated neural challenger with geodetic context}

The stronger model is delivered in two layers, the second gated. Layer~1 is the
region-refit tiled ETAS with the full hygiene pipeline. Layer~2 is a conditional
spatio-temporal neural point process with a Hawkes inductive bias, in the spirit of
RECAST~\cite{dascher2023} and FERN~\cite{zlydenko2023}: keep the additive background plus
summed-triggering skeleton, replace the fixed kernels with learned maps, and ingest a
gridded covariate field, here the GNSS strain-rate channel (other geophysical channels are
honestly zero-filled). A fit-time scalar anchors the integrated one-day forecast rate to
the training rate on the evaluation grid; without it the background head over-predicts the
absolute rate by roughly $490\times$ (a failure we diagnose and report in
Section~\ref{sec:geodetic}). The challenger reaches the public map only if it beats ETAS
in our prospective CSEP harness and is calibrated; until then, ETAS ships.

\subsection{Calibration and uncertainty (release blockers)}

The public probability is recalibrated by isotonic regression and validated with a
reliability diagram per horizon; an uncalibrated probability does not ship. The uncertainty
triad (optimistic P10, expected median, pessimistic P90)~\cite{schneider2022} is a genuine
epistemic-plus-aleatory decomposition sourced from ETAS parameter covariance, propagated
$M_c/b$ uncertainty, model-selection uncertainty, and \emph{over-dispersion}: because
regional seismicity has variance well above its mean, the pessimistic bound is wider than a
naive Poisson quantile (negative-binomial behaviour~\cite{kagan2017}). A pessimistic bound
that is merely a Poisson quantile systematically under-warns at the tail and is not
acceptable. In the highest-stakes window, immediately after a large mainshock, the daily job
uses an incompleteness-aware likelihood with a time-dependent $M_c(t)$, the failure mode
behind OEF-Italy's documented under-estimation of the 2016--2017 Central Italy
sequence~\cite{spassiani2023}.

\section{Evaluation: a leakage-free CSEP testbed}
\label{sec:eval}
% =====================================================================

A forecast is only as honest as its prospective scoring. The only scoring framework is
CSEP via pyCSEP~\cite{savran2022}; using standard tests, not bespoke metrics, is what lets
a reviewer dispute the model rather than the test code.

\subsection{Two representations and over-dispersion}

We emit both a gridded-rate forecast (Poisson expected count per space--magnitude cell) and
a catalog-based forecast (an ensemble of synthetic catalogs). Regional seismicity is
over-dispersed relative to Poisson, so the Poisson grid tests over-reject during sequences;
the catalog-based tests~\cite{savran2020} relax that assumption and are the correct primary
path for a clustered daily forecast, always reported paired with their Poisson counterpart.

\subsection{The forecast clock and five leakage modes}

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig-protocol.pdf}
\caption{The leakage-free forecast clock and the pseudo-prospective back-analysis. At each
daily issue $t$ the model receives only the conditioning slice up to $t$, the forecast is
sealed, and it is scored against the catalog as known at $t$; half-open windows tile the
timeline without double-counting. The back-analysis fits once and reconditions forward
across weekly windows (refresh parents, hold parameters), scoring IGPE per window and per
region under a pre-registered rule.}
\label{fig:protocol}
\end{figure}

The primary mode is pseudo-prospective~\cite{mizrahi2024}, driven by a forecast clock
(Fig.~\ref{fig:protocol}): at each daily issue time $t$ the model is handed only the catalog
slice up to $t$, the forecast is sealed, then the clock advances; the conditioning window is
half-open on the left and the target $[t,t+H)$ half-open on the right, so back-to-back
horizons tile the timeline without double-counting, and an always-on invariant asserts no
leakage before every fit. Five leakage modes are engineered against: temporal leakage
(the clock); catalog-revision leakage (an immutable input snapshot per issue makes each
forecast byte-reproducible); $M_c$-inconsistency leakage (one uniform $M_{\min}$ applied to
the model and every baseline); region/parameter snooping (pre-registered polygons, horizons,
thresholds, grid, and declustering, frozen across the test split); and multiple-testing
inflation (every region\,$\times$\,horizon cell reported, including failures). To keep the
multi-region back-analysis tractable the ETAS parameters are fit on a weekly cadence and
only the triggering conditioning is reconditioned daily, a measurement-preserving change
validated to a 0.05\% median deviation from full-refit-every-day.

\subsection{Consistency and comparison tests}

Consistency (calibration) tests are built on the Poisson joint log-likelihood over
space--magnitude bins, $L(\Omega\mid\Lambda)=\sum_i[-\lambda_i+\omega_i\ln\lambda_i-\ln(\omega_i!)]$:
the number (N) test with Poisson-tail quantiles $\delta_1=1-F(N_\text{obs}-1\mid N_\text{fore})$
and $\delta_2=F(N_\text{obs}\mid N_\text{fore})$, the magnitude (M), spatial (S), likelihood
(L) and preferred conditional-likelihood (CL) tests~\cite{schorlemmer2007,zechar2010}. These
are necessary but not sufficient: passing consistency calibrates one model but never
establishes skill, because a trivial ``aftershocks follow mainshocks'' model also passes.
Skill is established only by winning a comparison test against both the smoothed-seismicity
null and ETAS, measured as information gain per earthquake (IGPE, nats),
\begin{equation}
I_N(A,B)=\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N\big(\ln\lambda_A(k_i)-\ln\lambda_B(k_i)\big)-\frac{\hat N_A-\hat N_B}{N},
\label{eq:igpe}
\end{equation}
tested with the paired $t$-test $T=I_N/(s/\sqrt N)\sim t_{N-1}$ and corroborated by the
Wilcoxon W-test~\cite{rhoades2011}. The gain over ETAS measures genuine added skill
precisely because both models already reproduce Omori clustering. ROC/AUC is banned as a
skill metric: it is invariant to calibration and, on a rare per-cell-per-day task,
degenerates into a region classifier (the DeVries trap~\cite{devries2018,mignan2019}). Proper
scoring rules~\cite{gneiting2007} and a Molchan/area-skill view~\cite{zechar2008} are
reported only as communication aids. Ship rules are pre-registered before each run, and a
shuffled-label negative control guards against over-fitting.

\section{Related work: the honest ML-vs-ETAS verdict}
\label{sec:related}
% =====================================================================

As of writing, no machine-learning model has been shown to reliably beat a well-fit ETAS
for short-term forecasting under fair, prospective, CSEP-style testing, a requirement we
gate against rather than a claim that ML can never help. The decisive evidence is
EarthquakeNPP~\cite{stockman2026}: five modern neural point processes on California
1971--2021 with strict chronological splits and CSEP consistency tests, none of which
outperformed ETAS, and specifically the best neural model reached only about $68.6\%$ on the
spatial test where forecasting value lives, versus $92.0\%$ for ETAS. The methodological fix
was repairing a data-leakage flaw in earlier neural work (non-chronological splits inflate
metrics via triggering); once temporal splits and the large sequences are restored the
apparent advantage evaporates. The honest exemplars gain from ETAS gaps, not depth: RECAST
improves on temporal ETAS only for large training catalogs~\cite{dascher2023}, and FERN's
reported information-gain improvement came mostly from ingesting sub-$M_c$ events and learning
fault-aligned anisotropy, with the authors' own caveats (not CSEP-tested, no uncertainty
quantification) as release-blockers for us~\cite{zlydenko2023}. The geodetic input we study
is grounded in long-term global rate models: GEAR1 blends smoothed seismicity with a
strain-rate model~\cite{bird2015}, and prospective global hybrids find strain adds skill
globally while its regional short-term contribution is weaker~\cite{bayona2022}.

\subsection{ETAS adds skill only where there is triggering}
\label{sec:bias}

The first adversarial question is whether ETAS only looks good by over-fitting
high-seismicity margins. A 60-day, leakage-free, per-region back-analysis
(Table~\ref{tab:bias}, IGPE vs the Poisson null at one and seven days) answers it: ETAS adds
skill in the densest triggering regime (Japan, $+0.072$ at one day) and collapses to exactly
the null in low-seismicity interiors (Central US, Western Europe, Eastern Australia). A
single-window multi-model benchmark (Table~\ref{tab:bench}) tells the same story at larger
absolute scale and shows that the equal-weight ensemble of heterogeneous models
under-performs ETAS, because averaging in the null and the weak Reasenberg--Jones dilutes the
triggering signal that is the skill. Low-seismicity $0.0$ is not a failure; it is the correct
statement that a self-exciting model has nothing to add without sequences.

\subsection{ETAS-family stacking: a global gain that does not generalize}
\label{sec:stack}

The only prospective lever the literature supports over a single well-fit ETAS is a
score-weighted ETAS-family ensemble, by a small and often non-significant
margin~\cite{marzocchi2012,herrmann2023}. We build a convex, log-score-optimal stack of
three ETAS-family variants (base tiled, short-memory, long-memory), the smoothed null
entering only as each member's background, weights shrunk toward the base vertex, with a
pre-registered rule requiring a positive, CI-excludes-zero gain on the global view and in at
least two high-seismicity regions at seven days. The 14-window back-analysis
(Table~\ref{tab:stack}) earns a real, significant global gain ($+0.011$ over 751 events,
$13/14$ windows positive), carried by the long-memory variant capturing late-aftershock
structure the base cutoff misses; but only $1/4$ high-seismicity regions is positive
(California, on three events, noise). Under the pre-registered rule this is a
\textbf{no-ship}. A temporally adaptive variant appears to rescue it ($3/4$ regions positive)
but is refuted on scrutiny: it reduces the global gain, and the regional ``improvements'' are
sign-flipping noise on 3--54 events (no regional CI excludes zero), a multiple-comparison
artifact. The regional seven-day $M\!\ge\!5$ gate is under-powered; the global view is the
only adequately powered test, and there the static stack already gives the honest answer.

\subsection{The short-horizon failure is over-dispersion, not shape}
\label{sec:overdisp}

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig-overdispersion.pdf}
\caption{Over-dispersion-honest consistency on a 30-day global window. The frozen-intensity
forecast predicts $N_\text{fore}=64.3$; the full ETAS branching cascade lifts the mean to
$82.1$ ($+28\%$) with a wide count distribution, so the observed 96 is consistent. The
Poisson number test rejects (fail, $q\!=\!10^{-4}$); the catalog-based test does not (pass,
$q\!=\!0.13$). Read from \texttt{e13\_catalog\_based.json}.}
\label{fig:overdisp}
\end{figure}

Why does the base ETAS N-test fail on active windows? A catalog-based number test, an ETAS
branching-process forward simulation, gives the honest in-window count distribution
(Fig.~\ref{fig:overdisp}). On a 30-day global window the frozen-intensity forecast predicts
$N_\text{fore}=64.3$ (background 60.1 plus first-generation triggering 4.2), the full cascade
lifts the mean to 82.1 (Fano factor 1.8), and the observed 96 is consistent: the Poisson
N-test fails at quantile $10^{-4}$ while the catalog-based test passes at $0.13$. Two honest
findings follow. The apparent under-forecast is over-dispersion plus secondary cascade, not a
rate bias; and the frozen-intensity forecast systematically \emph{under-counts by about
$28\%$} because it omits within-window secondary triggering, so the catalog-based test is the
correct consistency tool. This is one 30-day global window; the method generalizes.

\subsection{The horizon-dependent value of geodetic context}
\label{sec:geodetic}

\begin{figure}[!t]

$4/8$ positive) but positive in every window at thirty days (mean $+0.078$). Read from

\caption{Mechanism of the horizon dependence. The calibrated neural's total forecast is
constant across windows (18.1 events at seven days, 77.3 at thirty, ratio $\approx30/7$): it

\label{fig:mechanism}

The headline concerns the strain-conditioned neural challenger. Diagnosing it required first
fixing a calibration failure: the raw model over-predicted the 30-day global rate about
$490\times$ (98{,}932 vs 248 observed) because the training compensator constrained the
background only at event locations. A fit-time rate anchor fixes it: the calibrated model
forecasts 218.9 events (ETAS 173.7, observed 248) and passes the N-test. Only the calibrated
model is scored below.

At the one-to-seven-day operational horizon the geodetic context does not beat ETAS
(Fig.~\ref{fig:horizonigpe}): an eight-window back-analysis gives mean IGPE vs ETAS $-0.053$,
positive in only $4/8$ windows, with high per-window variance (a single badly-missed
sequence window drives $-0.80$). The ETAS-family stack of Section~\ref{sec:stack} likewise
gains only $+0.009$ globally at seven days. The one-to-seven-day mean-rate IGPE is at or near
a practical ceiling.

At thirty days the same geodetic context beats ETAS robustly and regionally
(Fig.~\ref{fig:region}): a ten-window back-analysis gives a global mean IGPE vs ETAS of
$+0.115$ over 2166 events, positive in $9/10$ windows with a paired-$t$ lower bound of
$+0.087$, and positive in every high-seismicity region (Chile $+0.656$, Japan $+0.192$,
California $+1.007$, New Zealand $+1.210$). The mechanism (Fig.~\ref{fig:mechanism}) is
explicit: the calibrated model's total forecast is essentially constant across windows
(18.1 at seven days, 77.3 at thirty, ratio $\approx30/7$), so it deploys a time-flat geodetic
background rather than a triggering model. That is why it loses at seven days, where ETAS
triggering dominates the integral, and wins at thirty, where a better stationary background
dominates it. The operational consequence is honest: the one-to-seven-day product stays ETAS,
and the geodetic context is deployed only as a longer, background-dominated outlook.

% =====================================================================
\section{Discussion}
% =====================================================================

The four results cohere into one message: for global $M\!\ge\!5$ short-term forecasting, base
tiled ETAS is at or near the practical ceiling on mean-rate IGPE, and the honest levers lie
elsewhere. A separately conducted adversarial review of 2024--2026 frontier paths
(time-varying $b$, regime-gated neural routing, anisotropic finite-fault kernels,
magnitude-head networks, multi-resolution scoring) found that none survived skeptical
refutation as a large short-horizon gain, converging with the EarthquakeNPP
conclusion~\cite{stockman2026} and our own measurements. The value is not in chasing a larger
seven-day number but in scoring honestly (catalog-based over-dispersion tests,
Section~\ref{sec:overdisp}), deploying a covariate at the horizon where it earns skill
(Section~\ref{sec:geodetic}), and refusing to ship a result that does not generalize
(Section~\ref{sec:stack}). The methodological posture is as much a contribution as any single
number: pre-registered ship rules, shuffled-label negative controls, and the distinction
between a well-powered global test (751--2166 events) and an under-powered regional gate
(3--54 events) are what separate a real result from a multiple-comparison artifact, of which
the temporally adaptive stack, which a naive read would have shipped, is the clearest example.

% =====================================================================
\section{Honest limitations}
% =====================================================================

The pseudo-prospective back-analysis spans weekly windows over a limited recent period; the
event counts are large (2166 global events at thirty days) but the temporal span is short, so
the results are a well-powered snapshot rather than a multi-year prospective record. The
neural challenger's absolute calibration, though fixed for scoring, is not production-hardened,
and it is not used in the daily one-to-seven-day product. Only one context channel (GNSS
strain) is active; the remaining geophysical covariates are honestly zero-filled and not
claimed. The magnitude homogenization uses literature $M_w$ relations as a documented fallback,
with a bounded $b$-value bias the per-tile fit largely removes. The secondary-cascade
under-count is measured on one 30-day global window. None of these is hidden: each corresponds
to a flag or an artifact in the released code.

% =====================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% =====================================================================

We built a complete, honesty-first operational earthquake forecasting system and, on it, a
leakage-free multi-region CSEP testbed with pre-registered evaluation, and used it to measure
rather than assert four things: that ETAS earns skill only where triggering exists; that an
ETAS-family stack gains globally but not regionally; that the binding short-horizon
consistency failure is count over-dispersion with an approximately $28\%$ secondary-cascade
under-count; and that the value of a GNSS-strain geodetic covariate is horizon-dependent,
absent at one to seven days and robust at thirty, because the calibrated model contributes a
time-flat background rather than triggering. The practical recommendation is to ship calibrated
ETAS at the operational horizon, score with catalog-based over-dispersion tests, and reserve
the geodetic context for a longer background-dominated outlook. All code, configurations,
provenance manifests, and committed artifacts are released openly so that every number is
reproducible and every negative result is on the record.

\section{Per-region back-analysis detail}
The 60-day per-region back-analysis (Table~\ref{tab:bias}) uses the CSN, ComCat, JMA and GeoNet
catalogs for Chile, California, Japan and New Zealand respectively, with region polygons,
horizons, thresholds, grid and declustering pre-registered and frozen across the test split;
the low-seismicity interiors (Central US, Western Europe, Eastern Australia) are included
specifically to expose the high-versus-low bias. Each region\,$\times$\,horizon cell, including
the failures, is committed in \texttt{results/backanalysis-<region>-*.json}.

\section{Experiment register}
The system was built as an append-only register of experiments E1--E14, each recording a
motivation, a change, a measured result, and a justification, so no change to the model or
pipeline is silent. The results in Section~\ref{sec:results} correspond to E5 (per-region
bias), E9 (multi-model benchmark), E10--E11 (neural gate and pseudo-prospective horizon), E12
(ETAS-family stacking and its adaptive variant), E13 (over-dispersion and secondary cascade),
and E14 (30-day geodetic validation). The full register, with the tractability engineering
(closed-form Omori integrals, KD-tree neighbour queries, reconditioning, and vectorized neural
scoring) that made the multi-region back-analysis feasible, is in the repository documentation.
